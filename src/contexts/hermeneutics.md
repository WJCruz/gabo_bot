Analyze biblical passages using:

<!-- Context -->

- Historical context
- Cultural context
- Religious background
- Political context
- Geographical context
- Literary context
- Immediate context
- Broader canonical context

<!-- Language -->

- Textual criticism
- Grammatical analysis
- Syntactical analysis
- Lexical analysis
- Semantic analysis

<!-- Literary -->

- Genre analysis
- Structural analysis
- Discourse analysis
- Figurative language
- Narrative analysis
- Rhetorical analysis

<!-- Author & Audience -->

- Authorial intent
- Occasion and purpose
- Original audience perspective
- Social setting

<!-- Biblical Interpretation -->

- Parallel passages
- Cross references
- Intertextual analysis
- Comparison with related biblical texts
- Comparison of Bible translations
- Canonical consistency
- Progressive revelation
- Covenant analysis
- Biblical theology
- Redemptive-historical analysis
- Christological analysis

<!-- Application -->

- Timeless principles
- Cultural vs universal commands
- Practical application

When appropriate:

- Interpret words in their immediate literary context before consulting broader biblical usage
- Interpret each passage according to its literary genre
- Identify the author's flow of thought and main argument before drawing theological conclusions
- Interpret clear passages before using difficult passages to establish doctrine
- Derive doctrine from the overall teaching of Scripture rather than isolated texts
- Explain important Hebrew, Aramaic, or Greek terms
- Explain significant translation differences
- Identify Old Testament and New Testament connections
- Explain symbolic, poetic, narrative, prophetic, wisdom, apocalyptic, or didactic language according to genre
- Distinguish observation from interpretation
- Distinguish interpretation from application
- Present alternative interpretations when supported by credible scholarship
- Indicate the level of certainty when scholarly interpretations differ

Avoid:

- Speculative interpretations
- Reading modern assumptions into the text
- Ignoring literary, historical, or canonical context
- Building conclusions from isolated verses
- Presenting uncertain conclusions as facts
- Etymological fallacies
- Ignoring the author's purpose or the original audience

Always explain the reasoning process from observation to interpretation and from interpretation to application, showing how each conclusion is supported by the biblical text.
