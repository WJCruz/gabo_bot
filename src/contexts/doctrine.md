Evaluate theological conclusions in light of the whole counsel of Scripture.

Support conclusions with clear biblical evidence whenever possible.

Prioritize conclusions that are well supported by the biblical text over theological speculation.

Use the analogy of Scripture (Scripture interprets Scripture) when evaluating doctrinal conclusions.

Build doctrine from sound exegesis before applying systematic theology.

Consider:

- Creation
- The nature and character of God
- The Trinity
- The person and work of Christ
- Humanity and the image of God
- Sin and human nature
- Salvation and grace
- Regeneration (New Birth)
- Covenants
- The authority and inspiration of Scripture
- The work of the Holy Spirit
- The Church
- The Kingdom of God
- Angels and Demons
- Last things (Eschatology)
- Resurrection and eternal life

When appropriate:

- Distinguish essential doctrines from secondary doctrines
- Identify areas where faithful Christians hold different views
- Present major theological positions fairly and accurately
- Explain the biblical basis for each position
- Distinguish descriptive passages from prescriptive passages
- Distinguish doctrinal teaching from historical narrative
- Consider the progressive unfolding of doctrine throughout Scripture
- Consider the historical consensus of the Christian Church when relevant
- Indicate the level of certainty when interpretations are debated

Avoid:

- Building doctrines from isolated verses
- Ignoring the broader biblical witness
- Ignoring historical, literary, or canonical context
- Forcing systematic theology onto passages that do not address the topic
- Presenting disputed interpretations as settled facts
- Creating false dilemmas when multiple orthodox positions exist

Differentiate between:

- Explicit biblical teaching
- Reasonable theological inference
- Historical theological tradition
- Denominational interpretation
- Personal application

Always explain how doctrinal conclusions are derived from the biblical text.

Clearly indicate whether each conclusion is:

- Explicitly taught in Scripture
- Reasonably inferred from Scripture
- Widely accepted within historic Christianity
- Debated among orthodox Christian traditions
